This approach is a solid foundation for memorizing vocabulary because it leverages several core principles of cognitive science. By building a "stack" of words and testing yourself repeatedly, you are moving information from short-term to long-term memory.

Here is a breakdown of why this works, where it might struggle, and how you can refine it.

---

### Why This Process is Effective
* **Active Recall:** Instead of just reading the words, you are forcing your brain to retrieve the information. This strengthens the neural pathways associated with that word.
* **Scaffolding:** Starting with one word and adding more (1, then 1+2, then 1+2+3) prevents **cognitive overload**. It allows you to build confidence before the task becomes complex.
* **The "Sliding Window" Effect:** Once you reach 10 words and start "deleting one and adding one," you are practicing a version of a buffer. This keeps your brain engaged with a manageable set of data without feeling overwhelmed by an infinite list.

---

### Potential Drawbacks
* **The "Serial Position" Effect:** In a list of 10, humans tend to remember the first few words (Primacy Effect) and the last few words (Recency Effect) best. The words in the middle (positions 4 through 7) are the most likely to be forgotten.
* **Lack of Spaced Repetition:** Once a word is "deleted" from your active set of 10, when do you see it again? If you never see it again, the **Forgetting Curve** will eventually claim it.
* **Context Isolation:** Quranic words often have deep roots and meanings that change based on context. Memorizing them in a vacuum can sometimes make it harder to recognize them when reading a verse.

---

### How to Improve the Process

#### 1. Implement "Leitner" Style Spacing
Instead of just deleting a word when you reach 10, move it to a "Mastered" pile. Review that mastered pile once every 3 days, then once a week. This ensures the words stay in your long-term memory.



#### 2. Use the "Root" Method
Since you are studying Quranic Arabic, most words come from a **3-letter root**. When you add a new word to your game, try to group it with another word from the same root.
* *Example:* If you are learning *Kataba* (he wrote), add *Kitab* (book) shortly after. This creates a mental web rather than isolated strings of data.

#### 3. Visual and Auditory Anchoring
To make the recall more "sticky," try to include:
* **Audio:** Hearing the correct Tajweed/pronunciation while seeing the word.
* **Imagery:** Associating the word with a specific image or a famous verse where that word appears.

#### 4. The "Weight" System
Not all words are equal. If you miss a word during the quiz, it shouldn't be "deleted" after 10. It should stay in the rotation longer than words you get right instantly. This is the logic behind Spaced Repetition Systems (SRS).
